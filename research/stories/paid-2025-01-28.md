# #paid Success Story

## The Story

### Problem Discovery

Bryan Gold and Adam Rivietz discovered the market opportunity through personal observation:

**The Aha Moment:**
Their close friend, Ronnie Friedman (@inspiredtobefit), started an Instagram account to document her weight loss journey while struggling with depression and obesity. Within six months, she accumulated 75,000 followers, and brands began reaching out offering free products in exchange for sponsored posts.

**Key Insights:**
- Influencers with significant followings were giving away valuable promotional content for free
- Brands wanted to work with influencers but lacked systematic ways to connect
- There was no standardized platform to broker fair, paid deals between the two parties
- The friction in the influencer marketing process created inefficiencies for both sides

> "The inspiration came from our friend, Ronnie, who started a fitness journey and documented the progress on Instagram." - Bryan Gold

As Gold explains, Ronnie lacked the means to monetize his influence through brand partnerships despite having 100,000 followers. Adam and Bryan recognized this untapped opportunity and began brokering deals between Ronnie and various brands, realizing she could—and should—be earning money for those posts.

### MVP Development

**Timeline:** 2013-2014 (approximately 1 year)

**Initial Concept:**
Rather than allowing influencers to struggle finding marketing collaborations independently, Gold and Rivietz developed a platform functioning as a marketplace connecting content creators with brands seeking authentic endorsements.

**Initial Features:**
- Creator profiles with follower counts and engagement metrics
- Brand campaign posting system
- Basic matching between creators and brand campaigns
- Payment processing system
- Content rights management

**Tech Stack:**
- Platform: Web-based marketplace
- Matching: AI-driven image recognition and machine learning algorithms
- Psychographic matching: Beyond basic demographics to ensure audience alignment

**Development Process:**
The founders came from different backgrounds:
- Bryan Gold and Adam Rivietz grew up together in Richmond Hill, attending the same nursery, elementary, high school, and university
- They had prior entrepreneurial experience (Adam ran a prom limousine service in university)
- The platform was built to solve a problem they witnessed firsthand with their friend Ronnie

**Quality Thresholds:**
From early on, #paid set minimum quality standards:
- Creators needed at least 5,000 followers
- Maintained a 2% average engagement rate to qualify for brand collaborations
- This ensured brand partners received quality influencer partnerships

### Key Decisions

1. **Setting Quality Thresholds (5K followers, 2% engagement)**
   - **Why:** Ensure brand partners receive quality influencer partnerships; prevent platform dilution with low-quality creators
   - **Result:** Built reputation for quality over quantity; attracted premium brands willing to pay for proven engagement

2. **AI-Driven Matching vs Manual Curation**
   - **Why:** Scale beyond manual brokering while maintaining quality matches; use "advanced image-recognition and machine learning" for psychographic matching beyond demographics
   - **Result:** Differentiated from competitors; enabled efficient scaling to 50,000+ creators

3. **Focus on CPE (Cost-Per-Engagement) Metric**
   - **Why:** Move beyond vanity metrics (follower counts) to actual engagement results for brands
   - **Result:** Brands could optimize spending by "driving the most engagements (likes and comments) for the lowest cost possible"

4. **Targeting 25K-250K Follower Range**
   - **Why:** This "sweet spot" balances authentic engagement with reach; avoids mega-influencers (expensive, less engaged) and nano-influencers (too small reach)
   - **Result:** Created distinct market position in micro/mid-tier influencer space

5. **Expanding to TikTok and Multi-Platform**
   - **Why:** Creator economy expanding beyond Instagram; TikTok reached 3 billion+ installs
   - **Result:** Platform relevance maintained as social media landscape evolved

6. **Building End-to-End Workflow (Not Just Matching)**
   - **Why:** Eliminate all friction in influencer marketing process
   - **Result:** SaaS-enabled marketplace with automated payments, legal agreements, ROI reporting became comprehensive solution

## All Tactics & Strategies Used

### Tactic 1: Friend Validation → MVP Approach
- **What They Did:** Before building the platform, manually brokered real brand deals for their friend Ronnie who had 75K Instagram followers
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Identified friend Ronnie's untapped monetization opportunity
  2. Reached out to brands directly on Ronnie's behalf
  3. Negotiated paid partnerships instead of free product exchanges
  4. Learned what both creators and brands needed from the process
  5. Validated that brands would pay for authentic influencer content
- **Context:** Pre-launch validation phase; founders wanted proof of concept before investing in technology
- **Results Achieved:** Validated market demand; learned marketplace dynamics; proved brands would pay creators fairly; built confidence to pursue platform
- **Time Investment:** Several months of manual brokering
- **Cost:** Minimal (just time and outreach efforts)

### Tactic 2: Quality Threshold Strategy
- **What They Did:** Set minimum requirements of 5,000 followers and 2% average engagement rate for creators to join platform
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Analyzed which creator metrics correlated with brand satisfaction
  2. Set minimum follower count to ensure adequate reach
  3. Required engagement rate to ensure authentic audience
  4. Automated verification of these thresholds
  5. Maintained standards consistently as platform scaled
- **Context:** Differentiation strategy; preventing platform dilution with low-quality creators; ensuring brand ROI
- **Results Achieved:** Built reputation for quality; attracted premium brands; avoided race-to-bottom pricing; maintained brand satisfaction
- **Time Investment:** Ongoing automated verification
- **Cost:** Opportunity cost of turning away creators below thresholds

### Tactic 3: AI-Driven Psychographic Matching
- **What They Did:** Developed "advanced image-recognition and machine learning" algorithms to match influencers with appropriate brands beyond basic demographics
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Built image recognition to analyze creator content themes
  2. Developed machine learning models for psychographic profiling
  3. Matched based on audience alignment, not just follower counts
  4. Evaluated context beyond surface-level demographics
  5. Continuously refined algorithms based on campaign success data
- **Context:** Core competitive differentiator; addressing inefficiency in manual influencer selection; scaling beyond human curation capacity
- **Results Achieved:** Could efficiently match at scale (50,000+ creators); higher campaign success rates; differentiation from competitors; justified premium pricing
- **Time Investment:** Significant ongoing R&D investment
- **Cost:** Substantial engineering and ML expertise required

### Tactic 4: CPE (Cost-Per-Engagement) Metric Focus
- **What They Did:** Positioned platform around cost-per-engagement metric instead of traditional reach/impressions metrics
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Calculated actual engagement (likes, comments) per dollar spent
  2. Showed brands how to optimize for engagement vs reach
  3. Provided reporting on CPE across campaigns
  4. Used CPE as primary success metric in sales conversations
  5. Educated market on why engagement matters more than followers
- **Context:** Market was focused on vanity metrics (follower counts); brands wasting money on fake engagement; need for performance-based measurement
- **Results Achieved:** Attracted brands focused on ROI over vanity; justified fair creator compensation; demonstrated platform value clearly; built trust with data-driven approach
- **Time Investment:** Ongoing metric tracking and reporting
- **Cost:** Engineering resources for tracking and analytics

### Tactic 5: Incubator Leverage (Ryerson DMZ)
- **What They Did:** Participated in and graduated from Ryerson DMZ incubator program
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Applied and accepted to DMZ program
  2. Used resources, mentorship, and network during incubation
  3. Graduated successfully
  4. Leveraged DMZ alumni network for brand introductions
  5. Used DMZ credibility for early fundraising
- **Context:** Early-stage startup needing validation, resources, and connections
- **Results Achieved:** Credibility boost; access to brand connections; mentorship for scaling; network for fundraising; office space during early days
- **Time Investment:** Duration of incubator program
- **Cost:** Equity or fees (if any) to participate in DMZ

### Tactic 6: 25K-250K Follower "Sweet Spot" Targeting
- **What They Did:** Identified and focused on creators with 25,000-250,000 followers as the optimal range
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Analyzed campaign performance across follower tiers
  2. Found micro/mid-tier influencers had best engagement rates
  3. Positioned platform for this "sweet spot" range
  4. Avoided mega-influencers (too expensive) and nano-influencers (too small)
  5. Marketed platform to both creators and brands in this range
- **Context:** Differentiation from platforms chasing celebrity influencers; finding best ROI range for brands
- **Results Achieved:** Distinct market position; avoided direct competition with celebrity-focused platforms; delivered better ROI for brands; fair compensation for creators in growth phase
- **Time Investment:** Data analysis to identify sweet spot
- **Cost:** Opportunity cost of not serving mega/nano tiers

### Tactic 7: End-to-End Workflow Automation
- **What They Did:** Built comprehensive workflow beyond just matching—included automated payments, legal agreements, content rights, ROI reporting
- **How They Implemented:**
  1. Identified all friction points in influencer campaign lifecycle
  2. Built automated payment processing system
  3. Created standardized legal agreement templates
  4. Automated content usage rights management
  5. Developed ROI and sales impact reporting dashboards
  6. Integrated all steps into single platform workflow

- **Results Achieved:** Became "SaaS-enabled marketplace" not just matching platform; reduced friction; faster campaign execution; clear ROI demonstration; enterprise appeal
- **Time Investment:** Significant ongoing product development

- **Results Achieved:** Attracted major enterprise clients; higher contract values; longer customer relationships; differentiation from self-service competitors; $15.6M revenue by 2025 with 142 employees suggests significant services component

- **Context:** Scaling from 2 founders to 11, then 40, 80, and finally 142 employees
- **Results Achieved:** Founder time freed for strategic work; company could scale beyond founder capacity; team grew sustainably; company reached $15.6M revenue
- **Time Investment:** Ongoing hiring and team development
- **Cost:** Employee salaries and benefits (142 employees)
